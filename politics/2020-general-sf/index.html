I heard Trump was messing with the election; how do I vote?
  • Register to vote by October 19. Register as a Democrat (regardless of your political beliefs), because then you can vote in the Democratic presidential primary, and for the DCCC.
  • Due to the pandemic, everyone will receive a ballot by mail. You can check your ballot's delivery (and later, receipt) status here.
  • If you're done more than a week before the November 3 election, it's safe to return your ballot by mail. Otherwise, I'd recommend you drop off your ballot in person, at one of the ballot drop-off locations or at any polling location on Election Day.

The details

How'd you decide who to endorse?

I consulted campaign websites (mainly to find who endorsed local candidates); Ballotpedia, especially for CA propositions; my friends; and the YIMBY and SPUR voter guides, detailed below.

  • YIMBY Action: YIMBY (Yes in my backyard) Action is San Francisco's main pro-housing group. The primary political issue in the city, and California as a whole, is housing. YIMBY Action advocates for more housing, both legalizing market-rate apartments and funding subsidized housing.
  • SPUR: SPUR is the San Francisco Bay Area Planning and Urban Research Association, and they publish endorsements accompanied by detailed analyses of the San Francisco and California propositions.
  • Note: do not trust the SF League of Pissed Off Voters. The group is largely funded by the same incumbents they're presumably "pissed off" at. They represent the leftist, anti-market wing of politics in San Francisco.

The candidates

President and Vice President

You already know why you're voting for Joe Biden, but let me add that his housing plan is solid. He supports fully funding the Section 8 housing voucher program, a relatively cheap step that would dramatically reduce proverty. He also supports ending exclusionary single family home zoning, and creating a new renter's tax credit. I'm not optimistic Biden would actually, but he has the potential to move us in the right direction on housing.

U.S. Representative, District 12

I support Nancy Pelosi for reelection. She is the current Speaker of the House, and has enormous clout in Congress. Though I do wish we had a younger generation of leadership (the top 3 members of Democratic leadership in the House are all octogenarians), she's been effective in running the House, getting bills passed, and managing impeachment in a popular way.

Her opponent, Shahid Buttar, is a democratic socialist in the Bernie vein, whose record focuses on activism. As you can imagine, almost everyone has endorsed Pelosi, and she is almost certain to win.

State Senator, District 11

Scott Wiener is the best legislator I've ever known. He's been tireless in advocating for more housing, our state's biggest issue. On housing, Wiener passed SB 35, which has led to the under-construction 2000-home project in Cupertino at the site of Vallco, and tens of thousands of other units. Another obscure but critical housing bill he passed is SB 828 requiring cities to zone for their actual housing needs. It resulted in a quintupling of counties' housing goals in Southern California, and the Bay Area doubling its housing goals.

Among many other bills, Wiener wrote California's bill protecting net neutrality, and has been working to extend nightlife hours so that bars can be open until 4am.

By contrast, his opponent, Jackie Fielder, has a housing plan to spend $100B over 10 years to build 100,000 more homes. Unfortunately, we have a shortage of more than 3 million homes (we are 49th in housing units per resident), so this would barely make a dent in the crisis. Fielder opposes SB1, a $1B gas tax to fund transportation and transit (despite 83% support for it in SF, its incentive against driving gasoline vehicles, and its funding for transit), because it is "regressive" and "structurally makes dependent on more gas," a position emblematic of her tendency to focus on purity over pragmatism.

State Assembly, Districts 17 and 19

David Chiu and Phil Ting are both pro-housing Democrats. Neither are facing Democrats, and both are prohibitive favorites for reelection.

State propositions

Prop 14, stem cell funding: No

This proposition would renew funding for the California Institute for Regenerative Medicine (CIRM), which was created and financed by voters in a 2004 proposition, and has since run out of money. Prop 14 lacks endorsements from prominent CA politicians, and $5.5B is a lot of money. There's no clear rationale for this spending, and I think we could do a lot better with $5.5B (for scale, the entire SF Muni budget is $1.3B).

Prop 15, increase commercial property taxes: Yes

Prop 15 is a significant step in undoing the biggest mistake in CA political history, Prop 13. Under 1978's Prop 13, property taxes cannot go up with the value of the property; instead, they go up only 2% or inflation, whichever is lower. Imagine that in 1980, Apple bought a building in Cupertino for $5M, and an Apple employee bought a house for $50,000. Then, after 40 years of 2% growth, Apple pays 1% annual property taxes on $11M in assessed value, and the employee pays $1,100 in annual taxes on $110,000 in assessed value, even if the Apple campus is now worth $200M and the home is worth $2M.

This year's Prop 15 undoes Prop 13 for commercial properties, while retaining it for residences. It phases in beginning in 2022, and by 2026 would assess business property taxes based on property value. It is expected to generate ~$10B towards schools, community colleges, and local governments.

One negative effect of Prop 13 is that it's encouraged cities (for example, Bay Area suburbs) to build more office space than housing. This is because revenue from housing (property taxes) is capped by Prop 13, and while that applies to businesses as well, payroll and other taxes can be levied on businesses. Unfortunately, Prop 15 may exacerbate this imbalance, by making commercial property even more profitable for local governments. However, this potential drawback is not serious enough for me to oppose the measure.

Prop 15 has been endorsed by most prominent state politicians, as well as all major Democratic 2020 presidential nominees.

Prop 16, reverse affirmative action ban: No endorsement

This proposition would repeal a 1996 proposition which stated that the California government and universities cannot "discriminate against or grant preferential treatment to persons on the basis of race, sex, color, ethnicity, or national origin in public employment, public education, and public contracting." If passed, it would allow affirmative action programs to be reinstituted at the University of California system.

A lesser-discussed potential impact of Prop 209 would be on government jobs and contracts. As the legislature wrote in their statement placing Prop 209 on the ballot, its implementation "cost women and people of color over $1,000,000,000 annually in lost contract awards." A UCSC study finds that contract prices fell 5.6% after Prop 209, as state governments were no longer required to prefer minority-owned businesss. Conversely, a Berkeley Law study emphasizes the return of "good old boy" networks, in which well-connected, nonminority contractors had more influence in government contracting, in preventing equal access to minority businesses after the passage of Prop 209.

Prop 16 is endorsed by most state politicians, as well as SPUR and the ACLU of California.

Prop 17, allow parolees to vote: Yes

This proposition restores the right to vote for people on parole for felonies (though not those currently serving their sentence). I believe all adult citizens should have the right to vote, and am in favor of this proposition.

Prop 18, allow those who'll turn 18 by the general to vote in the primary: Yes

This proposition prepones your voting age so that anyone who'd be old enough to vote by the general election could also vote in the primary election. CA would join 18 other states in making this change. It makes sense to let someone vote for a complete primary/general cycle, and aligning eligiblity to that.

Prop 19, strengthen Prop 13 property tax loophole for those 55 and older in exchange for reducing its inheritability: Yes

Back to Prop 13 (see my intro above) -- one especially galling provision of it, added in subsequent amendments, is that it's inheritable. In California, we have a feudal system, in which if you were lucky enough to buy real estate years ago, your children (and even grandchildren!) can inherit not just your property but your artificially low tax rate. Prop 13's tax rate is inheritable not just on your primary home, but also on up to $1,000,000 of assessed value of other property. So if your San Francisco landlord owns 5 other apartments, currently worth a total of $8,000,000 but assessed at a tenth of that, her children currently can inherit the $72,000/year property tax break along with the apartments.

Prop 19 would get rid of this tax break; allowing Prop 13's tax rate to only be inherited on the primary home in which the inheritor resides. This is the reason I'm in favor: it reduces the inheritability of Prop 13.

The trade-off is that Prop 19 would actually increase the value of the Prop 13 tax break for those 55 and older. They are currently able to take their artificially low Prop 13 rates with them if they sell their home and buy another (a) in the same county and (b) for no more than their current home was worth. Prop 19 removes both of those restrictions. I believe older homeowners are huge beneficiaries of California's tax system, are generally very wealthy due to appreciation of their property values, and do not need additional tax breaks, and thus do not support this provision of Prop 19. However, one silver lining is that this generosity might encourage homeowners living in high-cost, high-opportunity areas of the state to move elsewhere, increasing the supply of houses in Cupertino, Palo Alto, and Berkeley (for example) available for younger folks in the workforce.

Prop 20, lengthen criminal sentencing: No

I don't support lengthening criminal sentences, and especially not removing the possibility of parole, as this proposition does for 51 crimes. This proposition is supported by the Republican Party and Devin Nunes, and opposed by the Democratic Party, former Governor Jerry Brown, and the ACLU of California.

Prop 21, allow localities to expand rent control: Yes

Since the 1995 Costa-Hawkins Act, cities in California have been heavily restricted in passing rent control laws. They're only allowed to impose rent control on (a) apartments, not single-family homes or condos (b) built before 1995 (or a city's existing cutoff date, 1979 for San Francisco).

Prop 21 would allow cities to impose rent control on (a) single-family homes, as long as they're owned by individuals who own at most 2 homes (b) which were built more than 15 years ago. New construction is exempt from rent control for 15 years (to allow developers to profit from the building) before it kicks in.

Another change is that under current law, when a rent-controlled tenant leaves, the landlord can raise rents to market rate. This incentivizes landlords to evict heavily rent-controlled tenants. Prop 21 allows cities to cap the rent increase when a vacancy occurs at 15% every 3 years. This may reduce the eviction incentive, but it could also incentivize landlords to sell the house to a new owner (who might live in it), or rent it out to their friends rather than the broader market.

The cause of high housing prices in California is that supply is too low because we haven't built enough homes. Prop 21 would not solve this problem. At the margin, it might worsen it, by reducing the profit developers would expect on their new construction. This is my biggest concern about this bill. However, I looked at the UC Berkeley study cited by SPUR to show Prop 21 would reduce housing construction, and the study drew its conclusion by asking industry experts, who said that even a 40-year exclusion of new construction from rent control would reduce the supply of new housing. While I understand why those in the real estate industry would resist any rent control, a 40-year exclusion seems like ample time to recoup investment on a property. Indeed, so much can change in a 40-year timeframe, from laws to the regional economy, that I find the conclusions implausible. At any rate, the far bigger obstacle to housing supply is that apartments are illegal to build in 70% of SF, as well as the vast majority of other residential land in California, and we should address zoning and other restrictions rather than using the supply concerns to block any expansion of rent control.

Ultimately, though I don't expect to personally benefit from rent control, I recognize its value for neighborhood stability. Expanding rent control might reduce the rancor between tenant groups and more pro-development groups.

Prop 22, overturn employee classification for gig workers: No

A 2018 California Supreme Court decision, codified into law by 2019's AB 5, restricted worker categories which could be classified as independent contractors. In general, I'd be in favor of reforming this law, which has had consequences including discouraging many national publications from hiring freelance journalists in California.

Uber and Lyft have responded by placing Prop 22 on the ballot. This proposition would overturn AB 5 for "app-based drivers," as well as specify some benefits and labor protections for them, for example partial health insurance payments depending on number of hours worked. One much-maligned provision of Prop 22 is an incredibly high 7/8th threshold for amending it in the legislature. As context, it's important to remember that by default voter propositions cannot be amended by the legislature at all (only by the voters in another proposition). This raises the question: should labor regulations by passed by ballot proposition, and written largely by the companies they'd regulate? My answer is no; it would set a bad precedent and be hard to amend in the future.

Another concern is what Uber and Lyft would do if Prop 22 fails and AB 5 is implemented. Personally, I don't believe they'd pull out entirely. In interview, Uber's CEO suggested Uber prices might go up by 20% in SF if Uber had to classify drivers as employees, which to me seems like a small extra price to pay. Even if Uber and Lyft were to pull out entirely, the California rideshare and delivery markets are large enough that I believe other companies would enter.

Prop 23, dialysis regulation redux: No

You may remember a similar dialysis-related proposition (2018's Prop 8, which failed). This proposition is the result of a labor dispute between a healthcare worker union and the duopoly of companies who run dialysis clinics in California. Don't spend any more mental energy on it -- any regulatory changes needed for dialysis should be done through the normal legislative process.

Prop 24, data privacy regulation: No

This proposition amends data privacy laws and establishes a new California Privacy Protection Agency. It's backed by a few politicians including Ro Khanna, David Chiu, Nancy Skinner, and Andrew Yang, but opposed by the ACLU of California and most media editorials. It was placed on the ballot after being filed by a random SF-based real estate developer and amends a California data privacy law that was passed recently, in 2018.

Given the complexity of this proposition, and how recently the law it's amending was passed, I default to voting no. Any changes ought to be made through the legislature, after the full impacts of the 2018 law have become clear.

Prop 25, eliminate cash bail: Yes

The cash bail system requires low-income people accused of a crime to turn to family or high-interest bail bond companies to post bail in order to return to their jobs and lives while awaiting trial. If someone is likely to reoffend or flee, it makes sense to hold them in jail; if not, the bail is unnecessary.

One concern I've heard about this proposition is that the "risk assessment tool" which would be used to decide whether to release offenders would be biased against, for example, Black people. The proposition states that the tools should be "demonstrated by scientific research to be accurate" and "minimize bias," which at least seems well-intentioned. While ML tools and algorithms can perpetuate existing bias and shouldn't be blindly trusted, I'd trust them more than the existing system, in which individual judges make subjective decisions.

Local propositions

Prop A, bond for parks and public services: Yes

This proposition issues bonds and directs $487.5M for projects including parks and open space, supportive housing and shelters, mental-health services, and street paving. It's supported by both Mayor Breed and the entire Board of Supervisors.

Prop B, reorganize public works: No

Matt Haney, who has championed this proposition, is rightly concerned about corruption in SF's Department of Public Works. This proposition would split out some of its responsibilities into a new Department of Sanitation and Streets, and create two new commissions to oversee the two departments.

SPUR has a great analysis here: they point out that separating the management of the construction of buildings from their maintenance is an antipattern, and that the new organization may reduce accountability for the departments, by splitting responsibility between the Mayor and the Supervisors (currently, the Mayor oversees the Department of Public Works).

Prop C, allow noncitizens on advisory boards: Yes

34% of San Franciscans are immigrants, and according to SPUR, as many as 13% are non-residents. Allowing all residents to serve on city commissions and boards will allow those bodies to be more representative of the population.

Prop D, police oversight: Yes

SF has both a city police department and a county Sheriff's Department; this proposition would strengthen oversight of the latter by establishing a civilian office to investigate misconduct, as well as an oversight board that could make recommendations to the Sheriff and Board of Supervisors.

There's a bit of complexity here; an alternative proposal would be to strengthen the oversight of the Department of Police Accountability (which investigates police misconduct) over the Sheriff's Department. That said, the new departments are not particularly expensive ($3M per year), and improve accountability as judged by SPUR.

Prop E, allow fewer than 1,971 cops: Yes

It turns out that the city charter requires at least we maintain at least 1,971 police officers. While removing this requirement doesn't require that SF reduce its police staffing levels, it at least creates the possibility, and it doesn't make sense for detailed staffing levels to be be mandated in the city charter.

Prop F, business tax changes: Yes

This proposition completes San Francisco's transition from a payroll tax (which can discourage a business from hiring workers) to a gross revenues tax (known as a gross receipts tax). The specifics are a compromise between the Mayor and the Board of Supervisors.

Prop F also temporarily reduces taxes on business sectors negatively impacted by the COVID-19 pandemic, and contains a technical feature to allow the city to more quickly spend revenue raised from previous, disputed ballot measures.

Prop G, reduce voting age to 16 for local elections: Yes

Sure; this change means that people will become eligible to register and vote while still in school, where there might be an organized institutional framework to encourage them. Currently, voting participation is lower (in some past cycles, less than half) among young voters compared with seniors. If this change increases youth voter participation, that's a meaningful benefit.

Prop H, expedite business permitting: Yes

It takes too long, and costs too much, to start a business in San Francisco. Even an ice cream shop in the Mission is taking months, and more than $150K in rent and other costs, to open. Prop H speeds up the review and inspection process to 30 days for certain businesses, and relaxes rules around pop-up retail, usage of parklet, and artistic/charitable facilities.

In an ideal world, these regulatory changes would be passed through the normal legislative process, but in San Francisco, the Board of Supervisors likes the complexity of the process. It gives the Board more levers with which to favor well-connected businesspeople, or kill projects they don't like. Unable to reach agreement with the Board, Mayor Breed decided to put this proposition on the ballot directly.

Prop I, double property sales tax for large sales: No

San Francisco's transfer tax (a tax charged when a property is sold) rate, currently 3% on properties above $25M in value, already exceeds that of other Bay Area and California cities. Prop I would double this tax rate on properties at least $10M in value. (The rate for properties under $10M in value, which increases from 0.5% to 2.25% as the value increases, would not change).

Property owners who sell to nonprofits would pay only 0.75% in tax, while those who sell to the city (eg, to build affordable housing) would be exempt from the tax, to encourage such sales.

The city controller's office report finds that the higher transfer tax would discourage construction of new properties, and development of existing ones, which would cost $50M in GDP and cost 625 jobs (both small in proportion to the overall GDP and jobs base). Conversely, it would potentially raise $200M annually, or less if the rate of property sales decreased.

SPUR recommends a No vote, warning about increasing tax rates during a recession, and a decrease in new housing production.

Prop J, parcel tax for schools: Yes

This proposition replaces one school parcel tax, which has been contested for legal reasons, with a slightly lower one. It was placed on the ballot by Mayor Breed

Prop K, permit (but not fund) 10,000 public housing units: Yes

Currently in California, Article 34 of the state constitution requires voter approval for the government to build public, low-income housing. Scott Wiener is working on repealing it, but meanwhile, this proposition is needed to allow the city to build public housing.

Sure, granting this authorization is a positive step. Note, however, that even if Prop I, which is intended to provide funding to actually build the homes, passes, its $200M in revenue would only pay for 400 homes per year, at the current cost of around $500,000/home. While I support additional affordable housing, we should be clear-eyed that we cannot afford to plug a significant fraction of our housing shortage with affordable housing. At the state level, we'd need $1.75T to build the 3.5 million homes we need, compared with a state budget of $200B.

Prop L, punitive tax on SF cos with highly-paid execs: No

This proposition, authored by Supervisor Matt Haney, would calculate the ratio of the highest paid employee of a company to its median San Francisco employee salary, and impose increasing punitive gross receipts tax rates (or payroll tax rates, for businesses not based in SF which pay payroll tax) when the ratio exceeds 100.

I have a baseline skepticism to populist-sounding measures. Flaws with this proposal include: this tax seems easy to evade (companies could structure executive compensation as fringe benefits). It could encourage companies with a minor presence in the city, who pay payroll taxes, to leave by increasing the cost of doing business here.

District Propositions

Prop RR, sales tax for Caltrain: Yes

This small, 1/8 cent sales tax would allow Caltrain to continue operating amid dramatic ticket revenue decreases during the pandemic.
